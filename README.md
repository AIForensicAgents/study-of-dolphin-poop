# Study of Dolphin Poop
A field-study repository for the careful, ethical, and (yes) occasionally hilarious study of dolphin scat — for educational and biological research purposes.

---

## Overview
This repository collects field notes, annotated photographs, metadata templates, and best-practice guidance for documenting dolphin fecal samples (scat, droppings, feces) encountered during marine surveys. The goal is to support non-invasive monitoring of diet, health, and ecosystem signals while maintaining high standards of safety and ethics.

Contents you can expect in this repo:
- Field photo library (`images/`) with metadata
- Observation templates (`templates/`) for standardized data capture
- Morphology and identification notes (`docs/morphology.md`)
- Protocol suggestions for safe, legal handling (`docs/ethics_safety.md`)
- Analytic starters (not destructive lab protocols) for diet and environmental indicators (`notebooks/`)

This is intended for educators, citizen scientists, and professional field biologists. Contributions are welcome (see CONTRIBUTING.md).

---

## Why it matters
Dolphin scat provides a wealth of information without requiring capture or invasive sampling:
- Diet composition (fish otoliths, squid beaks) reveals predator-prey relationships and local fisheries interactions.
- Health indicators (parasite load, undigested prey, unusual color/consistency) help monitor population health.
- Chemical and microplastic residues in feces can be early-warning signals of pollutant exposure.
- Non-invasive sampling reduces stress for animals and enables repeated monitoring over time.

Studying scat supports conservation, fisheries management, and educational outreach by turning a small, ephemeral sample into a big research opportunity.

## Morphology notes
When documenting morphological features, capture consistent, objective descriptors:
- Color: pale, tan, brown, dark brown, black; note any unusual colors (orange/red, green) and possible causes.
- Consistency: liquid/slurry, soft, formed, firm; note presence of floating oil or sheen.
- Shape & size: lumps, strings, blobs; approximate dimensions (length, width) or relative scale (e.g., next to a ruler or coin).
- Visible contents: fish scales, otoliths (ear stones), squid beaks, bone fragments, shell fragments, seagrass, mucus, parasitic worms.
- Buoyancy: floats, sinks slowly, sinks quickly — useful for interpretation of lipid/air content.
- Odor: document descriptively and sparingly (e.g., "strong fishy"); do not rely on smell for safety decisions.

Photograph samples from multiple angles, include a scale object, and record GPS/time metadata consistently.

## Habitat / Diet clues
Scat composition can often be linked to habitat and prey availability:
- Coastal vs. pelagic prey: frequent small fish scales and otolith fragments often indicate coastal schooling fish; larger bones/beaks may indicate pelagic prey or larger demersal species.
- Seasonal signals: presence of spawning-stage fish or seasonal squid beaks can identify temporal feeding patterns.
- Habitat indicators: sea grass fragments or shell grit suggest nearshore foraging; predominance of deep-water squid beaks may indicate foraging over deeper shelf areas.
- Human impact signals: plastic fragments, netting fibers, or contamination residues flag anthropogenic influence on feeding grounds.

Combine scat data with sighting location, behavioral observations (e.g., foraging dives), and local fishery records to strengthen ecological inferences.

## Ethics & Safety
Safety and ethics are paramount. This project emphasizes non-invasive observation and careful handling when sampling is necessary.

Key principles:
- Respect wildlife: do not chase, harass, or attempt to touch dolphins. Maintain legal viewing distances and follow regional marine mammal guidelines.
- Permits: many jurisdictions require permits for collecting biological material from protected species or within protected areas. Obtain required permits before sampling.
- Personal safety & PPE: always wear nitrile gloves, use forceps/tongs for handling, and minimize direct contact. Use eye protection if splashing is possible. Wash hands and decontaminate gear after fieldwork.
- Biosecurity: avoid transferring material between sites. Use sealed containers, single-use consumables when practical, and label samples with clear metadata.
- Chain of custody & data privacy: securely store sample metadata and respect data sharing agreements and sensitive location information for endangered populations.
- Do not culture or attempt to grow organisms from fecal samples in the field or without appropriate biosafety facilities and approvals.

If unsure about legality or risk, consult local marine mammal authorities or institutional biosafety officers before collecting.

## Future Work
Planned directions for the project:
- Standardize metadata schema (ISO 19115-inspired) for better interoperability.
- Expand the photo library with geotagged, time-stamped samples across seasons and regions.
- Develop non-invasive eDNA assays and collaborate with labs holding appropriate approvals to analyze diet and pollutant markers.
- Build an open-access classifier (computer vision + metadata) to triage scat photos into likely diet/habitat categories.
- Community outreach: field guides, classroom modules, and participatory monitoring protocols for citizen scientists.

Contributions, collaboration proposals, and data-sharing partnerships are welcome — please open an issue or submit a pull request.
